The Cusco Secondary Cities project is a comprehensive geospatial approach to data collection and generation for emergency preparedness and urban sustainability. The goal of this project is to link geospatial tools, expertise, and applications that address critical humanitarian needs for long-term planning and development.



Workshop: June 29 – July 3

The 5-day workshop launched the Office of the Geographer’s Secondary Cities project to map critical data in rapidly growing urban regions of the world. Cusco, Peru was selected for the initial project to create a demonstration focusing on data generation of human geography themes for emergency preparedness, security, and resilience. The goal of this workshop was to teach spatial thinking by exploring geospatial science through the integration of multiple geospatial tools. Students learned basic skills for field-based data generation, management and sharing as well as the fundamentals of generating geospatial data and cartography.

The workshop included 26 participants: Universidad Nacional de San Antonio Abad del Cusco - UNSAAC (9); Universidad Alas Peruanas -UAP (4); Cusco Regional government (7); the Ministry of Agriculture (1) and Instituto Nacional de Defensa Civil del Peru – INDECI (2). Participants had a broad range of backgrounds, came from numerous disciplines, and had a mix of GIS skills. University students were partnered with government representatives to share perspectives and experience and to establish teams for the follow up activities of the workshop.

This was a unique workshop for three reasons:

  1. demonstration and learning of how cutting-edge geospatial tools can be seamlessly integrated
  2. emphasis on hands-on, field-based data generation and analysis of human geography data for emergency preparedness, human security and resilience
  3. long term investment through follow up activities.
Additionally, the high level of interest of the workshop students fueled a highly interactive session enriched by the diverse backgrounds of the participants, the Ministry of the Environment, Ministry of Agriculture, Cusco Regional Government and University students. The workshop culminated in a team project and presentation demonstrating how the integration of technologies facilitates field data collection, sharing and analysis.

Outcomes

  • 6 field-based projects collecting data on neighborhoods of the National University of Saint Anthony the Abbot of Cusco
  • Preliminary data on thematic human geography data (link to Cusco Geonode)
  • Established teams of university and government representatives
  • Partnership with Office of the Geographer, ESRI, UNSAAC, US Embassy
  • Follow-up activities

Tools

Students were introduced to a suite of geospatial tools. Working with different tools, both industry standards (i.e. ArcGIS) and open source (i.e., OpenStreetMap), workshop participants were provided a broad array of choices. Additionally they learned about how these tools can be integrated for value-added products and flexible data collection techniques.

Data

  • Students used multiple data types and formats as well as generating their own data. Working with UN population data, students learned the nuances of working with attribute data, understanding spreadsheets and using simple formulas in Excel, joining tables in ArcMap, and symbolizing data for visualization.
  • Students were introduced to the fundamentals of remote sensing and used high resolution satellite imagery to collect data for emergency preparedness and urban sustainability. Students used Landsat imagery to assess land use patterns.
  • Students developed field data collection skills through using smartphones with GPS apps and Survey123. After creating a survey form using XLSform specification, students used Survey123 mobile app to collect data. Using both smartphones with GPS apps and hand-held GPS units, students could compare results and assess the different tools.
Data Sharing Essential to data for emergency preparedness are strategies for sharing these data. Students were introduced to the Cusco Geonode where data are discoverable and can be shared amongst multiple users. Additionally, students learned about the metadata capabilities of Geonode and the importance of following proper protocols and practices for good metadata that enhances sharing and discoverability.

Capstone Project

Using an earthquake as the scenario, students were tasked with collecting thematic data needed for emergency preparedness. The exercise was situated in the University area, focusing on the adjacent neighborhoods. Working in teams and assigned a specific neighborhood, students used their newly acquired field data collection skills to collect data on at least two human geography themes. The scenario for the project was to respond to an earthquake event by collecting thematic data needed for emergency preparedness based on human geography themes.
